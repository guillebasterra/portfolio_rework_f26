I sank back into our fire-escape-designated camping chair (major lease violation), and looked out at the view of downtown LA, glowing in the distance like the LED on a smoke detector. I crossed my arms to fend of the nighttime breeze. It was late, and the air between us felt heavy. 

My best friend's father was dying. We didn't know it then, but the clock had wound down to its final two weeks. When he pulled me out onto the fire escape that night and asked to talk, I prepared to experience his grief, to listen, and lend a shoulder. Instead, he turned to me, eyes clear and piercing in the dark, and asked: "What's been up with you?"

I tore my gaze off the city lights and looked up at him. "What do you mean?"

"You've been off. I can tell. I mean, tell me if I'm wrong, but I can tell. I had to bring it up."

I mumbled something about being tired, busy. That I was fine, really. But he didn't believe it, and he kept pushing. And he was right, of course.

### Living in fear

The truth was, I'd spent the latter half of 2025 anxious. I didn't have a job lined up. I needed to get a good GPA, and go to the gym everyday. I needed to read before bed. Scroll less. Do side projects. Go out and meet people. I did a lot of those things. I also beat myself up a lot when I didn't. 

I became anxious about my future, about school. Awkward at parties. Awkward with the cashier at Trader Joes. I was fine, but I was fine in the way that you are fine when your shirt has an itchy tag. You can stand it for a while, and then a little while longer, but eventually you just want to go home and take the damn thing off.

So my friend sat me down on the fire escape and asked me about it. And finally, when I admitted to having been this way, this is what he told me: His dad, who he admired above anyone else, was dying. His greatest fear was panning out. He was at his lowest point, ever. 

And somehow, in all of that, he was at the strongest, mentally, that he had ever been. This tumult, this grief, had drowned everything else out. He was scared of nothing, because the worst was already at hand. 

How silly we had been, over the past years of our lives. How concerned over such minutiae as school, our social lives, whether the interviewer had thought I was chill for the joke I'd made at the end of the Zoom call, whether my ex-girlfriend was telling people that *she* had broken up with *me*. These things had seemed important. And in some way, they had been, but how silly we had been to let them occupy our minds and cloud our moods!

In the throes of his grief, my friend's mundane fears had clarified, and he had pulled me out onto the fire escape to transmit a little of that energy. He reassured me that I had nothing to worry about, that I was smart and good-hearted and that I would figure things out. He urged me to go out and grab life by the horns, to throw all my anxieties and hesitation to the wind and live my life on full blast. 

That he would think of me at a moment like this in his life, and try to share some of his strength, was a profound act of love, and it was his generosity that moved me as much as the words that he spoke. I came out of that conversation like a man changed. Chin up. Indomitable.

I had been letting fear guide me, letting external expectations and pride poison my motivations. If I could notice that fear, I realized - pull it out of that musty place where it resided in my mind and hold onto it while it squirmed to be free - then, then it would stop controlling me. And with my fears in hand, I could do, and be, anything.

### Fear is the mind killer

In Dune, Frank Herbert writes the Litany Against Fear, in which Paul Atreides recites to himself: "I must not fear. Fear is the mind-killer." This was the mantra I learned to cling on to after that night on the fire escape. I realized that fear, indeed, was at the root of almost every one of my personal pitfalls and that most attributes I aspired to could simply be traced back to the absence of fear.

To be socially magnetic is to live without fear. We stop asking what am I doing here? or am I leaning against the bar correctly? We become unconcerned with rejection, or maybe amused by it. We see the person stood in front of us and are curious about who they are rather than what they are thinking about us. 

To be disciplined is to live without fear. In order to work hard, we have to have the courage to try at things. We need to view failure not as a reflection of our selves but as a stepping stone, such that we are not afraid to pick paths, walk down them, and see what happens. Procrastination is a fear problem, not a motivation problem. The tasks at hand are daunting because we are scared that we won't be able to accomplish them, or that they will take too long, that we will let other people down or ourselves down. It is fear that holds us back. 

To love and live passionately is to live without fear. We acknowledge the risk of being bruised by life and move forward anyways.

### Living without fear? Or managing it?

So there it was. Live without fear, and all my problems would be solved. It took exactly one (!) job interview for that illusion to collapse. 

Fifteen minutes ahead of my first round at Google I was, to put it simply, shitting bricks. I tried to summon my newfound philosophy on fear, to channel Paul Atreides, but the nerves coursed through me like an electric current and no amount of intellectualizing could shake them. I abandoned my last-minute prep, opened my little Field Notes journal, and tried to make sense of what I was feeling:

> Don't forget that the point is to FAIL. If I always do well I am not shooting high enough.

I realized that, in some way, the fear I was feeling was necessary. It meant that this was a decent goal. I was shooting for something in which failure was a very real and possible outcome, and at which I did not want to fail. If failure wasn't a possibility then I wasn't pushing myself. And since the thought of failure scared me I knew that, in some way at least, I wanted this. I was nervous, and that was good.

As Hunter S Thompson puts it, "Anything that gets your blood racing is probably worth doing." He's spot on, though he might have been talking about doing acid (still spot on?). He definitely wasn't talking about big tech software engineering interviews but whatever, ok, that is my life right now. 

### Back in control

Georges St-Pierre - one of the greatest UFC fighters of all time (and a very likeable guy) - admitted that before every single fight, he sat in the locker room in terror. He'd ask himself: "what the hell am I doing here? why did I choose to come back?" He'd play and replay scenes of himself losing in his head. 

Then he'd start hitting pads with his coach, warming up, and in his very demeanor, he would weave a lie to himself, his staff, everyone in the locker room. Like he wasn't afraid. Like he was a warrior, built for this moment. And then, stepping out into the bright lights of the octagon, he would win. 

Life rewards action, and confidence is not a prerequisite for action. I do not need to wait until I am unafraid. When I notice fear, discomfort, anxiety - that is exactly when I must act. 

I think, in many ways, this is what my friend was doing when he took me out onto the fire escape to talk. In the face of his father's illness, he had been more afraid than ever in his life. And yet, with everything being taken from him, he had had the ability to act generously, to think clearly. It is this strength and courage to act righteously in spite of fear, or fueled by it, that I am determined to develop. It is about acting fearlessly, more so than living fearlessly, because the fear is a necessary part of that self transcendence. Fear presents us the opportunity, the challenge, to act bravely and virtuously.

### A Life Unshackled

There are days now when my friend is angry at life, at God, at the unfairness of it all. People tell him that time will heal, and it makes him want to punch them in the face. The pain of grief, he tells me, is unbearable, and yet the last thing he wants to do is move on. 

I realize, seeing him, that my life has not even begun to test me. It is very easy to sit here and type out a neat prescription for courageous living when my biggest concerns right now is unemployment (and deportation, to be fair). 

Still, I think there is value in the lessons I have discussed here. I am now, at least, aware of what I want. Where before I was living unawares - guided by fear, as we spoke about - I seek now to live in the thick of it, to feel and experience to my full capacity, whether it be fear, wonder, happiness, or heartbreak. I have learned that I must let these feelings flow through me, engulf me, and allow them to calcify, as these are the riches that will make up my life. 

"I claim them all", says the Savage in Brave New World. I want God, and poetry. I want real danger, freedom, goodness, and sin. I want to be the bull, charging with his head down, splintering the fences and raising dust in the wind. But also the heron, circling on high above, floating in the breeze and watching it all unfold.

There's a quote from Zorba the Greek by Nikos Kazantzakis (criminally underrated) that captures the feeling:

> This is true happiness: to have no ambition and to work like a horse as if you had every ambition. To live far from men, not to need them and yet to love them. To have the stars above, the land to your left and the sea to your right and to realize of a sudden that in your heart, life has accomplished its final miracle: it has become a fairy tale.

To me, this is a life unshackled from fear: a fairy tale. Beautiful, moving, and true. The world is yours to feast upon, in all its joys and sorrows. 

This year I aspire to act without fear. I aspire to use fear as my compass. When I feel it, I will walk into it. I will talk to the person sat next to me on the train. I will work like a dog, get a job that I am under-qualified for. Run. Sweat. Do hard things. I will write stupid articles on Substack. 

And so on.
