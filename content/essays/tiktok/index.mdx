When Instagram is on my phone, it’s automatic: I open the app on reflex, I end up on Reels, and my screen time goes up. A lot. I have the prescience to realize that this is bad for me, so I go through phases. I delete the app, I feel better, more productive. Then, a couple of weeks later, I find an excuse to download it again. And so on. 

At this point, the harm of short form content is well covered, but I wanted to take a look at two quotes from two great writers - Sylvia Plath and Albert Camus - to see if we can understand on a deeper level why short form content, in it’s current state, is harmful. 

The standard arguments - about dopamine levels, attention span, and time-wasting - are scary, but not particularly compelling. There is more to it, questions we have to answer about why we scroll and what it might be doing to us.

The first quote I want to talk about is a classic: Sylvia Plath’s fig tree.

“I saw my life branching out before me like the green fig tree in the story. From the tip of every branch, like a fat purple fig, a wonderful future beckoned and winked. One fig was a husband and a happy home and children, and another fig was a famous poet and another fig was a brilliant professor ... I saw myself sitting in the crotch of this fig tree, starving to death, just because I couldn't make up my mind which of the figs I would choose. I wanted each and every one of them, but choosing one meant losing all the rest, and, as I sat there, unable to decide, the figs began to wrinkle and go black, and, one by one, they plopped to the ground at my feet.”

We’ve all sat under Plath’s fig tree, yearning for life and yet overwhelmed by it. She describes a universal anxiety, in which we let every possibility in our lives rot for want of courage to choose. 

I was thinking about this the other day, and I realized: What is doomscrolling, really, if not Plath’s fig tree dilemma amplified, digitized, and weaponized? Rather than confront the discomfort of daily life and anxieties of choice, we stall, we sit at the base of the fig tree, starving to death but entertained. Scrolling is endless indecision, a forfeiting of choice, both because you are literally not choosing what you watch and, more importantly, because you are putting off real choices in your real life. As you let the hours pass, the ‘figs’ that are possibilities in your life drivel up and die. 

Each swipe teases us with a glimmer of life’s possibilities, 15 second snippets of humor, joy, hatred, ambition, love, and everything in between. That’s why using these apps is so addictive. In our anxiety over what path we will follow and what life we will live, we gorge on these clips, hoping they will fill that hole. Ultimately, however, and quite tragically, scrolling offers only a fleeting sedative, a cheap satisfaction to subdue our anxiety. It leaves us unsatisfied, wanting more. We are spectating, not living, and we want so desperately to live. 

So we return, again and again, scrolling restlessly in the hope that one day we might smash open the screen, crawl through, and finally inhabit the glowing moments that we are fed on our ‘For You’ page.

Plath never got to experience the modern wonder of algorithmic feeds and engagement driven content, but the anxiety she describes in her fig tree metaphor is at the heart of why we scroll today.

So what can we do? 

Put the phone down and go outside. Who would have thought?

Camus puts it a little better:

“You will never be able to experience everything. So, please, do poetical justice to your soul and simply experience yourself."

What I like about this quote, specially in response to Plath’s fig tree, is that it pushes you to accept whatever comes at you. Just go out and live - stop worrying so much. When Camus urges us to “do poetical justice to your soul and simply experience yourself,” he’s inviting us to turn inward, to find the life that’s already unfolding in our own bodies and thoughts.

Doomscrolling stops you from experiencing yourself. When you scroll, you are engaged with what's in front of you, but you are not involved. There is no observation onto the self, and that is a big problem.

That awareness of the self, this almost detached observation that I am here and what is this thing we call life and what do I want to do with it, is an intrinsic, if not essential part of living. Camus might even say that this itself is life. 

Abusing short form content by scrolling for hours a day distracts you from yourself, numbs you, takes you away from what it means to be you. 

Get off Tik Tok, delete Instagram, go outside and look at the sky or sit in your room and stare at the wall or talk to someone you love or read a good book or go take a shit and bring nothing with you but your bare ass.

Because that’s what it’s all about.

Note from December 14, 2025: When I wrote this article, I didn’t know how to end it, and so I decided so go with “Get off TikTok and go take a shit without your phone.” 

I was not at my most eloquent. 

I don’t love this ending because it is preachy, when really I am the one dealing with this issue - it's why I thought to write the article in the first place. So I’ve decided to come back to it and rethink the ending.

I saw a clip of Ethan Hawke recently where he mentions an old adage: “The voice of your spirit is extremely gentle”. Your intuition will guide you, but you have to pay attention, you have to be in touch with yourself to hear it. As covered in this article, doomscrolling stops you from experiencing yourself. It drowns out the whispering of your spirit. So, if I will give any advice, it is this: try to find moments to let your spirit whisper, and be there to hear it. We often let our days go by in a haze. Try, as much as you can, to stop that, to be intentional. Add color to your life, intentionally - play an instrument just because you love it, have a conversation with a stranger, wake up early one morning to catch the sunrise. Try, as hard as you can, to avoid looking for that color by consuming it through outside sources. Create it. Bring it out from within you.

That is what I have been trying, and while I still very often fall into the trap of my phone, I have generally been moving in a better direction.
